Open Resource Topic Atlas

Ten source-first classroom topics built from public-domain books and B132 open fragments. Each topic begins with source material, then turns it into speaking and writing moves.

10 topic seeds public-domain friendly B2-C1 speaking + writing source-first

How to Use It

Pick a topic, read the source cue, then make students build an answer using the speaking move and writing move. These are not lecture notes; they are launch pads for richer discussion.

1. Tact vs Brutal Honesty

Question: Is honesty still a virtue when it becomes needlessly hurtful?

Source cue: Twain asks us to compare an “injurious truth” with an “injurious lie.”

Class angle: students usually say “honesty is good.” Push them to distinguish truth, cruelty, tact, and social responsibility.

Speaking move: I value honesty, but I do not think honesty gives someone permission to be cruel.
Writing move: The moral value of truth depends partly on intention and effect, not merely on factual accuracy.
Twain, “On the Decay of the Art of Lying”Open fragment
The Ladies’ VaseOpen fragment

2. Public Opinion and Conformity

Question: How much of what we call “choice” is actually social pressure?

Source cue: Mark Twain writes that public opinion can force some men to do “anything.”

Class angle: useful for school uniforms, social media, cancel culture, exam pressure, and “everyone is doing it” logic.

Speaking move: I would not say people are completely controlled by society, but social approval can quietly shape their choices.
Writing move: Public opinion functions as an invisible form of pressure because it rewards conformity and punishes deviation.
open-pg-70 — Twain, What Is Man? and Other EssaysOpen original
open-pg-70579 — The philosophy of Elbert HubbardOpen original

3. Pride, Vanity, and Social Image

Question: Do people lie to protect others, or to protect the image they want others to have of them?

Source cue: Austen separates pride from vanity: one concerns self-opinion, the other concerns what others think.

Class angle: excellent for Instagram, grades, resumes, friendship, and the pressure to look successful.

Speaking move: Some lies are less about kindness and more about managing one’s public image.
Writing move: Social image can turn honesty into a performance rather than a genuine commitment to truth.
Austen, Pride and PrejudiceOpen fragment

4. Courage and Fear

Question: Is courage the absence of fear, or a way of moving through fear?

Source cue: Barrie presents courage as something to pray for, work for, and live with light-heartedness.

Class angle: strong for oral exams, stage fright, speaking English, and students who are afraid of making mistakes.

Speaking move: Courage does not mean I feel no fear; it means I refuse to let fear make the decision for me.
Writing move: The ability to act under pressure is more realistic, and perhaps more admirable, than the fantasy of being fearless.
open-pg-10767 — J. M. Barrie, CourageOpen original

5. Education and the Present

Question: Should education prepare students for exams, for work, or for the present life they are already living?

Source cue: Whitehead warns against depreciating the present and treating education as dead knowledge.

Class angle: perfect for GSAT/GEPT writing about education, pressure, relevance, creativity, and usefulness.

Speaking move: Education should not only prepare us for the future; it should also make the present more meaningful.
Writing move: Knowledge becomes powerful when students can connect it to the problems and experiences of the present.
open-pg-77011 — Whitehead, The Organisation of ThoughtOpen original

6. Habit and Self-Control

Question: Are we governed more by decisions or by habits?

Source cue: Schopenhauer and other essayists treat habit as a force that can quietly govern life.

Class angle: useful for phone use, study routines, procrastination, health, and language-learning discipline.

Speaking move: A habit is powerful because it turns a choice into something we no longer notice choosing.
Writing move: Long-term improvement often depends less on dramatic motivation than on small repeated patterns.
open-pg-10714 — Schopenhauer, essaysOpen original

7. Fiction and Truth

Question: Can something invented reveal a truth that plain facts cannot?

Source cue: Wilde argues that art and fiction can be more than factual copying.

Class angle: use this for novels, movies, AI-generated stories, memoir, social media persona, and “based on a true story.”

Speaking move: Fiction can be factually false and still emotionally true.
Writing move: A narrative may distort facts, yet reveal patterns of desire, fear, or behavior more clearly than a report.
Wilde, The Decay of LyingOpen fragment
Wilde, art vs natureOpen fragment

8. Work, Usefulness, and Success

Question: Is success about recognition, usefulness, money, or inner discipline?

Source cue: public-domain essays repeatedly connect work with character, social value, and public judgment.

Class angle: good for career goals, cram school, university choice, and the difference between being impressive and being useful.

Speaking move: I think success should include usefulness, not just visibility or status.
Writing move: A society that confuses success with attention may reward display more than contribution.
open-pg-70579 — The philosophy of Elbert HubbardOpen original

9. Kindness, Civility, and Respect

Question: Is politeness just fake niceness, or can it be a serious moral skill?

Source cue: The Ladies’ Vase treats true politeness as the outward form of a refined and kind mind.

Class angle: strong for classroom manners, disagreement, online comments, service workers, and family communication.

Speaking move: Politeness is not always fake; it can be respect made visible.
Writing move: Civility matters because disagreement becomes more productive when people feel respected rather than attacked.
The Ladies’ VaseOpen fragment
Austen, frankness and civilityOpen fragment

10. Freedom and Responsibility

Question: When does freedom become more than “doing whatever I want”?

Source cue: MacSwiney connects freedom with public duty, difficult choices, and responsibility.

Class angle: suitable for school rules, social media speech, political discussion, adulthood, and moral courage.

Speaking move: Freedom is meaningful only when people are willing to accept responsibility for its consequences.
Writing move: A mature view of freedom balances individual choice with public duty and the impact of one’s actions on others.
open-pg-13132 — MacSwiney, Principles of FreedomOpen original
