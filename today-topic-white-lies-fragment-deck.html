White Lies Fragment Deck

A classroom deck of short public-domain fragments for speaking and writing. Each card tells you exactly what to do: read a source fragment, borrow one idea, then turn it into a claim, example, or counterargument.

How to Use This Deck

1. PreviewOpen a fragment in the reader. Do not start with your opinion yet.
2. BorrowTake one useful idea: tact, credibility, privacy, omission, frankness, or social image.
3. AnswerUse the sentence move to build a spoken answer or a short writing paragraph.

Warm-up: trust, credibility, and social lying

Lying as a lost social art

Mark Twain · On the Decay of the Art of Lying · provocation

ironyprovocative thesistact

Cue: “the art of lying”

Use this when you want a spicy opening: Twain makes lying sound like a social skill, but the joke forces us to ask what tact is for.

Try saying:The problem is not only whether people lie, but whether they use language with tact, restraint, and social awareness.
Original source

The long-term cost of losing trust

Aesop · The Shepherd’s Boy and the Wolf · accessible opening

credibilityreputationtrust cost

Cue: “no believing a liar”

Use this for a simple but powerful first distinction: a lie can damage not only one fact, but the speaker’s future believability.

Try saying:A lie is dangerous because it weakens the trust that future truth depends on.
Original source

Core Debate: truth, tact, privacy, and harm

Judgment matters more than the label

Mark Twain · ethical gray zone · C1

judiciouswhite liemoral judgment

Cue: “judicious lying”

The useful word here is judicious: it moves the debate away from “lie = bad” and toward limits, intention, and consequences.

Try saying:A white lie may be defensible when it is limited, judicious, and meant to prevent unnecessary harm.
Original source

When truth becomes a weapon

Mark Twain · central debate card · B2-C1

brutal honestyharmtact

Cue: “injurious truth”

This is the strongest classroom pivot: truth does not automatically become virtuous if it is used carelessly or cruelly.

Try saying:Honesty loses its moral force when it becomes a way to hurt rather than to help.
Original source

Tact as kindness made visible

Anonymous · The Ladies’ Vase · concept definition

politenesssincerityrespect

Cue: “true politeness”

This route helps students avoid the lazy idea that politeness is always fake. Politeness can be a social form of respect.

Try saying:Tact is not the opposite of honesty; it is honesty shaped by kindness.
Original source

False politeness vs real tact

Anonymous · The Ladies’ Vase · contrast set

false politenesssurface mannerssincerity

Cue: “true politeness” vs surface manners

This card helps students separate empty performance from tact that grows out of a kinder attitude.

Try saying:Politeness becomes hollow when it is only a performance and not a sign of genuine regard.
Original source

A small falsehood to protect privacy

Jane Austen · Pride and Prejudice · case study

privacysocial pressuresmall lie

Cue: “little falsehood”

This gives students a realistic category: not every small lie is manipulation; sometimes the truth is private rather than morally owed.

Try saying:A small falsehood may be understandable when it protects privacy rather than distorts an important decision.
Original source

C1 Extension: omission, fiction, self-image, and frankness

The silent lie: deception by omission

Mark Twain · advanced nuance

omissionconcealmentdeception

Cue: “silent lie”

This is where the class gets interesting: can silence become dishonest when it is designed to create a false impression?

Try saying:Omission becomes deception when silence is used to create a false impression.
Original source

Healing, humane, or manipulative?

Mark Twain · writing framework

taxonomyintentioneffect

Cue: “healingly”

Use this as a vocabulary ladder: healing, humane, charitable, selfish, manipulative, malicious.

Try saying:The moral difference lies in whether the lie is healing and humane or selfish and manipulative.
Original source

Can fiction reveal truth?

Oscar Wilde · The Decay of Lying · creative extension

fictiontruthimagination

Cue: “fiction in the form of fact”

Wilde lets students move beyond morality: some invented things may reveal human behavior better than flat factual reporting.

Try saying:A story can be invented and still reveal something accurate about human behavior.
Original source

Art can be truer than raw nature

Oscar Wilde · The Decay of Lying · aesthetics route

artnaturetruth

Cue: art reveals what nature lacks

Use this when students are ready for a bold idea: a made-up thing may be more meaningful than unshaped reality.

Try saying:Art does not merely copy reality; it can organize experience into a clearer truth.
Original source

A fine lie as imaginative performance

Oscar Wilde · paradox and advanced register

paradoxstyleimagination

Cue: “a fine lie”

This is a high-register card for students who enjoy paradox: Wilde treats lying as style, invention, and artistic confidence.

Try saying:Wilde treats lying not as moral failure but as a display of imagination and style.
Original source

Self-image vs social image

Jane Austen · lexical nuance route

pridevanityimage

Cue: “pride” vs “vanity”

This card explains why people often lie: not only to protect others, but to protect the image they want others to hold.

Try saying:Many social lies protect not the listener’s feelings but the speaker’s desired image.
Original source

Frankness needs civility

Jane Austen · speaking role-play

franknesscivilitydirectness

Cue: frankness with restraint

A strong card for oral practice: direct speech is not automatically rude, but it needs timing, self-awareness, and civility.

Try saying:Frankness becomes admirable only when it is balanced by civility and self-awareness.
Original source
