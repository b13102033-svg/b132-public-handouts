2. Context reading: kindness, truth, or self-protection?

People often describe white lies as small, harmless lies told to protect someone’s feelings. A student may tell a classmate that a weak presentation was “fine.” A friend may avoid saying that a new haircut looks strange. A child may hide a broken object because telling the truth seems embarrassing. In each case, the lie looks small, but the motive is not always simple.

One side of the debate says that honesty is the foundation of trust. Aesop’s warning about the liar who is not believed “even when he speaks the truth” suggests that every lie has a future cost. Once credibility is damaged, even sincere words become harder to trust. From this view, a white lie is dangerous not because it is dramatic, but because it quietly weakens the habit of truthfulness.

Another side says that truth without tact can become cruelty. Twain’s contrast between “injurious truth” and “injurious lie” forces us to ask whether truth is still virtuous when it is used as a weapon. The question, then, is not simply “Should we tell the truth?” but “How should we tell the truth when another person’s dignity is at stake?”

A third possibility is more uncomfortable: sometimes white lies protect the speaker more than the listener. Austen’s distinction between pride and vanity points to self-image. We may claim to be kind, but we may actually be avoiding awkwardness, conflict, or the risk of being disliked. That is where tact begins to look like cowardice or manipulation.

Quick check

  1. Which source gives the strongest warning against lying? Why?
  2. Which source makes honesty seem more complicated than “always tell the truth”?
  3. When does tact protect another person, and when does it protect the speaker?

5. Writing tasks: use the readings, not only your opinion

Prompt A｜GSAT-style guided writing

Your school magazine is collecting short essays on the topic “Is a white lie ever acceptable?” Before writing, read at least two source fragments and one In Use page. In your essay, include:

  1. a clear position on white lies;
  2. one example from school, friendship, family, or online life;
  3. one idea from a source quote;
  4. one sentence using concession or contrast.

Suggested opening: The debate over white lies is not simply about truth and falsehood; it is about how language affects trust, dignity, and social relationships.

Twain Aesop In Use: concession

Prompt B｜GEPT / IELTS-style argument

Some people believe it is always better to be completely honest, while others think small white lies can be acceptable in social situations. Discuss both views and give your own opinion.

Use at least four items from the language bank: take issue with, bear in mind, root cause, desired effect, betray trust, make the case, draw a conclusion.

6. Practice: upgrade weak answers

  1. 改寫 thesis：White lies are good. → 改成 C1：有條件、有界線、有理由。
  2. 加 concession：I believe honesty is important. → 加上 “That said, ...”
  3. 情境口說：Your friend asks whether their speech was good. It was weak. What do you say now? What do you say tomorrow?
  4. 反駁：有人說 “A white lie is still a lie.” 你要承認哪一部分？反駁哪一部分？
  5. 來源整合：Use Twain to complicate honesty, Aesop to warn about trust, and Austen to discuss self-image.
  6. 寫作：用 150-180 words 回答 Prompt A；高程度學生用 250 words 回答 Prompt B。
