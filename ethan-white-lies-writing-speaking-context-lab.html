White Lies: honesty that hurts, kindness that hides.

This lesson turns one ordinary question into a richer debate: when does a “small lie” protect someone’s dignity, and when does it damage trust? Read the source windows, collect useful language, then build a speaking answer and a short argumentative essay.

Opening dilemma: choose your version of honesty

Your friend has just finished a presentation. The main idea was interesting, but the structure was hard to follow. Your friend asks, “Was it okay?”

A. White lie “Yeah, it was great!” Fast comfort, weak truth.
B. Tactful truth “Your idea was strong, but the structure was hard to follow. I can help you tighten it.” Truth plus care.
C. Brutal truth “Honestly, it was confusing.” Clear, but possibly cruel.
Pick one answer above, then use Twain, Aesop, Austen, or the In Use pages to defend or revise it.

4. Short reading: three ways to frame the topic

People often describe white lies as small, harmless lies told to protect someone’s feelings. A student may tell a classmate that a weak presentation was “fine.” A friend may avoid saying that a new haircut looks strange. A child may hide a broken object because telling the truth seems embarrassing. In each case, the lie looks small, but the motive is not always simple.

One side of the debate says that honesty is the foundation of trust. Aesop’s warning suggests that every lie has a future cost: once credibility is damaged, even sincere words become harder to believe. From this view, a white lie is dangerous not because it is dramatic, but because it quietly weakens the habit of truthfulness.

Another side says that truth without tact can become cruelty. Twain’s contrast between harmful truth and harmful falsehood forces us to ask whether truth is still virtuous when it is used as a weapon. The question is not only “Should we tell the truth?” but “How should we tell the truth when another person’s dignity is at stake?”

A third possibility is more uncomfortable: sometimes white lies protect the speaker more than the listener. Austen’s distinction between pride and vanity points to self-image. We may claim to be kind, but we may actually be avoiding awkwardness, conflict, or the risk of being disliked.

  1. Which source gives the strongest warning against lying?
  2. Which source makes honesty more complicated than “always tell the truth”?
  3. When does tact protect another person, and when does it protect the speaker?

5. Answer models: one idea, two levels

B2 spoken answer

I think white lies can sometimes be acceptable, but only when they are used to protect someone’s feelings and not to hide a serious problem. For example, if a friend’s presentation is weak, saying “It was terrible” may be honest but not helpful. A better answer is to be tactful: mention one strength and then give one clear suggestion.

Moves: clear opinion · simple example · tactful alternative
opinion language tact source

C1 argumentative answer

I take issue with the idea that honesty is morally valuable in every form. A truth can become injurious when it is delivered mainly to humiliate rather than to help. That said, a white lie is not automatically kind: if its root cause is fear, vanity, or conflict avoidance, it may betray trust in the long run. The fairest position is tactful honesty: protect dignity, but still offer an explanation that lets the other person improve.

Moves: challenge a claim · concession · motive analysis · balanced conclusion
